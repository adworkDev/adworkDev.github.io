Digital and Traditional Advertising – The Best of Both Worlds for SMEs

Posted on May 19 2021 · media advertising · adwork

The digital advertising industry has grown by leaps and bounds ever since the Internet became more accessible to the global public in the 2000s. With the increase in mobile phone usage and digital platforms, SMEs are spoilt for choice when it comes to advertising online, and so, traditional media advertising such as television, radio and newspapers is often not their first choice when they want to promote their business.

Source: Edel Corrigan; 29 Mar 2021, Malaysia Ecommerce Insights, www.eshopworld.com

Data in the chart above shows that the Internet as an advertising medium has experienced a sharp1 growth in recent years, as seen from the increase in total advertising expenditure.

Despite the rise of digital platforms and digital advertising, traditional media such as outdoor and in-store advertising spend also increased; and throughout the eight-year period, television and print media (i.e. newspapers and magazines) still remained a large part of media advertising. This is likely due to businesses realising the need to balance their exposure on both digital and non-digital media advertising platforms.

What does this mean for SMEs and can they have the best of both worlds?

In today’s hyper-connected environment where consumers’ attention spans are extremely short, SMEs can no longer afford to simply broadcast their products/services and prices if they hope to stand out from the crowd. To remain competitive, SMEs must find ways to create a relevant and compelling brand image and give their target demographic meaningful content that resonates with them.

SMEs should also consider leveraging traditional media, which is still considered a trustworthy source by many people. While the perception that traditional media is expensive is true to a certain extent, this is mainly because media owners had previously heavily depended on media buying companies for revenue and offer preferential rates to these companies.

Hence, direct media advertising bookings were often more expensive than bookings from media buying agencies who would buy media space and airtime from major media owners and resell it to market, be it multinational companies, SMEs, or even other advertising agencies. This often caused SMEs to be priced out from traditional media.
